Lesson #1 - Remote Login with SSH

Your first lesson is simply logging into the server with SSH (secure shell). On a Mac, this is easy. You just open a Terminal and type: ssh tc@localhost -p 2222. When it challenges you for the password, use foo. It may ask whether you want to accept the secure key the first time, and you answer yes. But then, you're logged in! It's that easy, because you really are on Unix. Use Spotlight to find Terminal if you need.

If you're on Windows, download Microsoft Terminal from the Windows store. The advice used to be to use PuTTY, but oh how times have changed. Soon after your Levinux experience, I expect most Windows users will want to install the Microsoft Windows Subsystem for Linux (WSL). But finish your Levinux experience here. SSH into here. Use vi to edit this file. And after you're satisfied you want to go down this rabbit hole, DrinkMe to install Linux on your Windows system with a lovely JupterLab install.

Congratulations! If you merely logged into Levinux with an SSH program, you are well on your way. Your next step will be editing this very HTML file. Sounds simple, right? But I will be introducing you the text editor vi, which will separate those who will be able to make the transition to the short stack from those who will not.

Lesson #2 - Editing HTML with vi

An introduction to vi(m)

Abandon all hope, ye who enter here. When people say "Anything worth doing is hard", they were talking about vi. But if you take a few things I say on faith, follow my step-by-step instructions precisely, deal with the frustration that will inevitably occur, I promise you will get over the hump and this will change your life. If you're becoming a high-tech superpower, then vi is your ability to fly.

Of all things in the "short stack", vi (and the iMproved version vim) are by far the strangest. But vi is part of the Unix standard, meaning it is always there. And of all things in technology, the one thing you will always be doing from now to forever into the future is editing text files. Your choice of the correct text editor and mastery over it makes all the difference.

vi plays a lot like a video game. As you master the key combo language like 2dw (delete 2 words), it's like you're gaining near telepathic control of text. But remember, vi is NOT A WYSIWYG editor. It comes from an age long before the Mac/Windows revolution when you had to dial into computers at 300-baud from "dumb terminals". A program written to be powerful in even that resource-starved environment really lets you fly in today's world.

Specifically, you can't just load vi and start typing. You have to hit "i" to go into insert mode, and when you're done, you have to hit the [Esc] key to get out of insert mode. And when you're not in insert mode, every keypress has a special meaning. At first, this is baffling and infuriating. In time, you will see the profound power and wisdom of this approach. Until then, get used to hitting the [Esc] key to deal with your frustration.

Onto the actual lesson

I'll assume you finished lesson one, and you have Terminal or PuTTY window with a successful login prompt that looks like: tc@box~$. If so, do the following:

  1. Type: cd htdocs and hit Enter. This will put you in the htdocs directory.
  2. Type: ls and hit Enter. This should list favicon.ico, index.html and style.css.
  3. Type: sudo vi index.html and hit Enter. This will load index.html into vi.
  4. Type: /Beta and hit Enter. This will jump your cursor to the word Beta.
  5. Type: i (don't hit Enter). This will put you in insert mode.
  6. Type: Hello World plus one space to insert some text.
  7. Hit the [Esc] key to get out of insert mode.
  8. Type: :w and hit Enter. This will write the file to the drive.
  9. Type: :q and hit Enter. This will quit out of vi.
  10. Refresh this page and see your edit!

Congratulations!

Whether or not you understood a bit of what you just did, you just entered a very elite class of techies—those who use vi. Fun fact: you could have combined steps 8 & 9 into :wq to write and quit. Commands can chain-up in vi, either saving you some keystrokes, or combining to yield some entirely new mind-boggling effect.

In addition to color-coding, you'll have spell-checking, undo, macros, and a wealth of other enhanced features that will make it the perfect programming environment for Python, and the next lesson.

Bonus Material...

That "sudo" you were typing up there meant "do as superuser" and let you have write privileges to the .html file. If you followed lesson #2 and didn't use the sudo command, you wouldn't have been able to save your edit—a super-common and frustrating mistake. If you want to put yourself into superuser mode so you don't have to always be typing sudo, the command is: sudo su. You will know this worked, because the character at the end of your prompt will change from $ to #. The hash symbol tells you you're in superuser mode. Typing "Exit" will bring you back out again. But while in superuser mode, your folder will change to /root, and you will have to cd back into /home/tc/htdocs, from where you could just vim index.html directly.
